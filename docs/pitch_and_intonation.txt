Pitch and Intonation

Pitch is the perceived highness or lowness of a sound, determined by frequency (Hz). Concert A4 is standardized at 440 Hz. Each octave doubles the frequency: A3 = 220 Hz, A5 = 880 Hz.

Intonation refers to pitch accuracy — playing in tune. Poor intonation is one of the most common issues in beginning and intermediate players, especially on fretless string instruments, brass, woodwinds, and voice.

Equal temperament divides the octave into 12 equal semitones. Each semitone is a ratio of the twelfth root of 2 (~1.0595). This is the tuning system used by most modern instruments.

Cents: Pitch deviation is measured in cents. 100 cents = 1 semitone. Playing 20+ cents sharp or flat is noticeably out of tune to most listeners.

Causes of poor intonation:
- Instrument not properly tuned before playing
- Embouchure tension or inconsistency (wind/brass/voice)
- Finger placement (fretted vs. fretless strings)
- Temperature changes affecting instrument resonance
- Poor listening habits — not hearing the target pitch before playing it

Ear training exercises: Sing or hum a pitch before playing it. Practice long tones while tuning to a drone. Compare your pitch to a reference tone and adjust in real time.

Vibrato is a controlled oscillation in pitch used as an expressive technique. Width and speed of vibrato should match the musical context — wide and slow for Romantic music, narrow and fast for Baroque.

Practice tip: Use a chromatic tuner or tuning app to visualize pitch accuracy objectively. Practicing to drones (sustained root notes) trains the ear to hear and correct intonation faster than tuners alone.
