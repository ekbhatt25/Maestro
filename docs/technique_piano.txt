Piano Technique

Piano technique encompasses posture, hand position, touch, and mechanical control of the instrument.

Posture and bench height: sit at a height where the forearms are roughly parallel to the floor or slightly downward. Sit toward the front of the bench. Feet flat on the floor or on pedals.

Hand position: curved fingers, relaxed wrist, arm weight transmitted through the fingertips. Avoid flat fingers, collapsed knuckles, or raised wrists.

Touch and tone production: the piano produces sound through hammer velocity — how fast the key is depressed. Tone is controlled by key speed, not pressure after the key is down. A heavier arm weight produces more volume; lighter touch produces softer tone.

Legato: smooth connection between notes. On piano, true legato is achieved by holding the first note until the second is played, with no gap and no overlap. Pedal can assist but should not substitute for finger legato.

Staccato: short, detached notes. Produced by a quick release of the key. Practice staccato scales and exercises to develop finger independence.

Scales and arpeggios: essential piano technique. Thumb crossings must be smooth — the thumb passes under the hand (not over) during ascending scales. Arpeggios require a flexible wrist to facilitate the thumb cross over larger intervals.

Chord playing: drop the arm weight into chords rather than striking. Voicing — balancing which notes are louder within a chord — is a key expressive skill. Typically, the top voice (melody) should sing out.

Pedaling: the sustain pedal should change with harmonic changes to avoid muddiness. Technique: press after attacking the keys, release on the next chord change. Half-pedaling allows partial sustain for nuance.

Common issues:
- Tension in the shoulders, forearms, or wrists
- Uneven finger strength (4th and 5th fingers typically weaker)
- Rushing runs and fast passages
- Overuse of the sustain pedal obscuring harmony

Practice tip: Hanon and Czerny exercises build finger independence and evenness but must be practiced musically — with attention to tone and dynamics, not just speed.
