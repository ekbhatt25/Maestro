Vocal Technique

The voice is a wind instrument — breath is the fuel. Vocal technique begins with breath support and builds up through resonance, placement, and style.

Breath support (diaphragmatic breathing): the diaphragm is the primary muscle of breathing. On inhale, the belly expands outward (not the chest). On exhale, the abdominal muscles engage to maintain air pressure. Shallow chest breathing leads to weak, unsupported tone.

Posture: stand or sit tall. Chin level with the floor. Shoulders relaxed and back. Tension in the neck and jaw is the enemy of free vocal production.

Registers:
- Chest voice: lower range, rich and resonant, felt in the chest. Most speech occurs here.
- Head voice: upper range, lighter quality, resonates in the head and sinus cavities.
- Mixed voice: blend of chest and head registers, avoids the "break" between registers. Goal of classical and contemporary training.
- Falsetto: a disconnected, breathy head voice. Natural in some styles (pop, R&B); avoided in classical.

Passaggio (the break): the transitional area between chest and head voice. Typically around E4-G4 for tenors, D4-F4 for sopranos. Navigating the passaggio smoothly is a primary goal of vocal training.

Vowel modification: in the upper range, vowels must be modified (opened or covered) to maintain resonance and ease. "Ah" becomes slightly more open; "Ee" needs jaw space to avoid tension.

Intonation: the most common pitch problem is flatting on long notes (dropping below pitch as support fades). Maintain breath engagement through the full duration of notes.

Warm-up exercises: lip trills (lip rolls), sirens (smooth glide from low to high and back), five-note scales on "mah" or "may," staccato scales on "huh" to activate breath support.

Common issues:
- Singing from the throat rather than the breath
- Tension in the jaw, tongue, or larynx
- Pushing chest voice too high instead of transitioning to mix
- Flat vowels reducing resonance
- Not warming up before rehearsal or performance

Practice tip: Record every practice session. The voice sounds different internally than externally. Listening back reveals pitch, tone, and diction issues that are invisible while singing.
