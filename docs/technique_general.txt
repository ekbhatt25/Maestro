General Practice Technique

Effective practice is deliberate, focused, and structured. Mindless repetition reinforces mistakes; deliberate practice targets and corrects them.

The fundamentals of deliberate practice:
1. Identify a specific problem (not "I'll practice the song" but "I'll fix measures 12-16 where I rush")
2. Isolate the problem — practice just that section
3. Slow down below the point of failure — find the tempo where you can play it correctly
4. Gradually increase speed using a metronome
5. Reintegrate into the full context

The 80/20 rule of practice: spend most of your time on the hard parts, not the parts you already play well.

Chunking: break difficult passages into small chunks (2-4 bars). Master each chunk, then combine adjacent chunks. This is more effective than running the whole piece repeatedly.

Mental practice: visualizing the physical movements of playing activates similar neural pathways as actual practice. Useful for memorization and rehearsing away from the instrument.

Practice session structure:
- Warm-up (10-15 min): scales, arpeggios, long tones, or other technical exercises
- Main focus (20-30 min): targeted work on a specific piece or skill
- Review (10 min): run familiar material to maintain it
- Cool-down: easy, enjoyable playing

Overuse injuries: muscle strain, tendinitis, and repetitive stress injuries are common among musicians. Warning signs: pain, tingling, numbness, or weakness. Prevention: take breaks (5-10 min every 45 min), warm up properly, avoid tension in the hands, arms, and shoulders, and never practice through pain.

Memorization: learn music in small sections, away from the page. Test yourself by starting at different points in the piece. Understand the structure (form, harmony, phrase structure) to retain it more reliably.

Practice tip: Keep a practice journal. Write down what you worked on, what improved, and what to focus on next session. Even brief notes dramatically improve the quality of practice over time.
