Rhythm and Timing

Rhythm is the arrangement of sounds and silences in time. A strong sense of rhythm is foundational to all music performance.

Tempo is the speed of the beat, measured in beats per minute (BPM). Common tempos: Largo (40-60 BPM), Andante (76-108 BPM), Allegro (120-156 BPM), Presto (168-200 BPM).

A metronome is the most effective tool for developing timing accuracy. Practice with a metronome at 60-70% of the target tempo before increasing speed. Rushing (playing ahead of the beat) and dragging (falling behind the beat) are the two most common timing errors.

Subdivisions: Breaking the beat into smaller units improves rhythmic precision. Quarter notes divide into eighth notes, which divide into sixteenth notes. Feeling the subdivision internally — even when not playing it — produces more consistent timing.

Syncopation places emphasis on weak beats or between beats. Common in jazz, funk, and Latin music. Practice syncopated rhythms slowly, counting the subdivisions aloud.

Polyrhythm is the simultaneous use of two or more conflicting rhythms. A common example is 3 against 2: one hand plays three evenly spaced notes while the other plays two in the same time span.

Groove refers to the feel and flow of rhythmic patterns. It often involves slight intentional deviations from strict metronomic time — leaning into beats, laying back, or pushing forward — to create musical tension and release.

Practice tip: Record yourself and listen back. Timing errors that are hard to feel while playing become obvious on playback. Clap or tap along to recordings of professional musicians to internalize their rhythmic feel.
